## Problem Statement

Nigerian residential estate communities (gated communities, housing estates) collect
monthly or annual security levies and other community dues from residents. Today, this
process is entirely manual: residents do bank transfers and send WhatsApp screenshots
as proof of payment. The Treasurer manually reconciles 50-100 screenshots per levy cycle
against a spreadsheet. Expenses are recorded informally or not at all.

The result: no audit trail, constant disputes about who paid what, and — most critically
— estate chairmen and Treasurers get falsely accused of stealing community funds because
there is no transparent, resident-visible record of what was collected and what was spent.

The platform replaces this chaos with a three-tier SaaS: the startup (User 1) manages
platform administration; estate managers/chairmen (User 2) collect levies and record
expenses; residents/landlords/tenants (User 3) pay levies online and view the full
financial picture.

## Demand Evidence

- Founder is the active Treasurer at their own residential estate in Nigeria. Has
  firsthand experience of the manual WhatsApp-screenshot-to-spreadsheet reconciliation
  process every levy cycle.
- Has spoken to other estate managers and chairmen outside their own estate. Confirmed
  the problem is widespread and the manual workflow is universal.
- Core pain confirmed: "Residents accuse them of stealing" — the lack of a paper trail
  means any discrepancy becomes a personal accusation against the chairman.
- No external paying customer yet — pre-revenue. Founder is domain-expert-turned-builder.

## Status Quo

Current workflow in a typical Nigerian estate:
1. Chairman announces levy due via WhatsApp group
2. Residents make bank transfers to a community account
3. Residents send screenshot of transfer receipt to a WhatsApp group or the Treasurer
4. Treasurer manually checks 50-100 screenshots against bank statement
5. Treasurer updates a spreadsheet with paid/unpaid status
6. Expenses are either unrecorded, logged in a notebook, or mentioned verbally at
   community meetings
7. At AGM, residents challenge expense figures — chairman has no audit trail to defend

The workaround costs: 4-6 hours of manual work per levy cycle per Treasurer,
recurring disputes at community meetings, and a persistent trust deficit between
residents and their elected executives.

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge

**Target user:** The estate chairman (or elected Treasurer) of a small-to-mid Nigerian
residential estate (30-150 units). This is typically a working professional who is
also a volunteer community leader. They are accountable to 50-200 residents. If money
goes missing or records are disputed, they bear personal reputational (and sometimes
legal) risk.

**What gets them in trouble:** Residents accuse them of stealing when there's no paper
trail to prove otherwise.

**What they need:** Not faster payment collection — they need a system that proves they
didn't steal. The expense log and resident-visible transparency layer is the core
product, not the payment collection feature.

**Narrowest wedge:** An estate manager tool that creates an immutable, resident-visible
ledger of what was collected and what was spent. Everything else (online payments,
SMS reminders, Excel import) layers on top.

## Constraints

- Nigeria market: bank transfer is the dominant payment method, not credit card
- Users (chairmen) are non-technical volunteers — UI must be simple
- Small estates (30-150 units) cannot afford high SaaS fees
- Mobile-first usage: most users access via smartphone
- Phase 1 notifications: email only via Resend (simpler, faster to integrate than SMS)
- Local payment processor required: Paystack or Flutterwave
- Competition: Gate Africa (10,000+ Nigerian users) exists but likely frames the
  product as "estate management" not "accountability infrastructure"

## Premises

1. The primary buyer is the estate chairman/manager (User 2), not residents
2. The core value is accountability/trust, not payment speed or convenience
3. Transaction fee on processed payments is the primary revenue model; flat estate
   subscription is a fallback if in-platform payment routing stays below ~70%
4. Gate Africa exists but there is a gap in the transparency/expense-accountability angle
5. Local payment rails (bank transfer via Paystack/Flutterwave) must be supported
   from day one

## Cross-Model Perspective

Independent Claude subagent (cold read, no prior session context):

Steelman: "This is a B2B compliance and accountability tool disguised as a payments
product. The real wedge is that estate chairmen are personally exposed to fraud
accusations with zero protection today. A platform that creates an immutable,
resident-visible ledger doesn't just save time — it shifts the political dynamics of
community governance. That's a defensible moat Gate Africa almost certainly hasn't
built deeply, because payments companies optimize for transaction volume, not trust

Key insight: "The chairman isn't buying software — they're buying personal protection
from a recurring social threat. That changes the sales pitch (lead with 'protect your
reputation,' not 'save time'), the pricing power (people pay more to avoid

giving up your audit history)."

Challenged premise #3 (transaction fee model): "Transaction fees only work if payments
flow through your platform. In Nigeria, bank transfer is culturally dominant and
technically trivial to do off-platform. You could end up with estates onboarded but
zero transaction revenue." → User agreed and revised premise to hybrid model.

48-hour prototype suggestion (noted for reference): Chairman-facing app with levy
ledger, expense log, and shareable public summary URL. Residents view without logging
in. Tests whether chairmen share the transparency link before building payment rails.

## Approaches Considered

### Approach A: Transparency-First MVP
Pure ledger + expense log. No online payments. Test chairman adoption before building
payment infrastructure.
- Effort: S (2-4 weeks) | Risk: Low | Completeness: 5/10
- Pros: Fast, proves core thesis, no payment compliance overhead
- Cons: No transaction revenue, chairmen may want payment collection before adopting

### Approach B: Full Three-Tier Platform (CHOSEN)
Build all three user tiers simultaneously with levy payments via Paystack/Flutterwave,
Excel resident onboarding, expense tracking, and platform admin panel.
- Effort: XL (3-5 months, 2-3 engineers) | Risk: High | Completeness: 9/10
- Pros: Complete product, transaction fee revenue active from day one, professional
  enough for institutional estate managers
- Cons: Months before testing, high risk of building wrong features before PMF

### Approach C: Chairman Tool + Public Link
Minimal chairman-facing ledger + expense log + shareable public URL. Payment links
(Paystack) added in Phase 2.
- Effort: M (3-6 weeks) | Risk: Low-Med | Completeness: 7/10

## Recommended Approach

**Approach B: Full Three-Tier Platform** (user's choice)

Build the complete platform across all three user tiers. Recommended build order:

**Phase 1 — Core Trust Layer (weeks 1-4)**
- Responsive web app (mobile-first — chairman and residents access via mobile browser)
- Chairman (User 2) dashboard: levy ledger, expense log with receipt photo upload
  - Expense approval: chairman posts expense (immutable once posted); amounts >₦200,000
    require a second confirmation step (audit deterrent)
  - Single admin in Phase 1: chairman only. Multi-role support (treasurer, vice-chairman)
    deferred to Phase 2 after core chairman workflow is validated.
- Resident (User 3) read-only view: their payment status, expense list (line items without
  individual resident names), community balance (total collected minus total expenses)
- Shareable public summary link — shows total collected, total spent, net balance, and
  expense categories. Does NOT show individual resident payment status (privacy).
  No login required. Designed to be pasted into WhatsApp groups.
- Manual payment recording: chairman uploads bank screenshot, marks payment received.
  System stores timestamp and photo as audit trail.
- CSV resident onboarding: columns = [unit_id, name, phone, email (optional), role
  (landlord/tenant)]. Duplicate phone numbers rejected. Unit_id must be unique per estate.

**Phase 2 — Payment Integration (weeks 5-8)**
- Paystack/Flutterwave payment flow for residents (online card or bank debit)
  - VERIFY FIRST: confirm Paystack/Flutterwave support real-time split settlement
    (fee to EstateOS, remainder direct to estate account). If not, EstateOS needs a
    middleware escrow wallet — budget an extra sprint.
  - Fee structure: transaction fee (1-1.5%) deducted from resident payment at source.
    Estate receives remainder. EstateOS does not hold community funds.
- Email receipts via Resend on successful online payment (SMS via Termii deferred to Phase 3)
- Bank transfer reconciliation (manual): resident uploads screenshot in app. System
  extracts amount + date for chairman review. Chairman confirms or rejects. No auto-match
  — Nigerian bank data is too sparse (name variations, truncated references) to auto-match
  reliably. Flag: "3 unreviewed transfers" banner for chairman.
- Dispute handling: resident can mark a payment as "disputed," upload proof. Chairman
  reviews and marks resolved or escalated. No automated arbitration — chairman is final.
- Email levy reminders via Resend: sent to all residents on levy announcement; weekly
  reminder to residents unpaid 14+ days past due. Chairman-configurable.
  SMS reminders via Termii deferred to Phase 3.

**Phase 3 — Platform Admin + Scale (weeks 9-14)**
- Platform admin panel (User 1): estate onboarding review (verify: legal entity name,
  bank account proof, estate address, resident count estimate); approve/suspend/activate;
  revenue dashboard showing transaction volume per estate
- Multi-levy type support: chairman defines N levy types (security, maintenance,
  generator, water) each with separate amounts, due dates, and payment tracking
- Latency check: test Vercel performance from Lagos before commit. If p95 latency >400ms,
  evaluate Railway or Fly.io with Africa-region deployment.

**Transaction fee:** 1-1.5% per online payment processed through the platform.
**Revenue decision tree:**
- Month 1-3: track % of levy payments processed online vs. manually recorded.
- If >70% online by month 3: stay on transaction fee model.
- If <70% online by month 3: evaluate moving to flat estate subscription
  (₦15,000-30,000/month by estate size). Decision date: end of month 3 for first cohort.
- Enforcement note: out of scope for Phase 1-3. No automated legal notices or service
  suspension. Manual follow-up by chairman remains the enforcement mechanism.

## Open Questions

1. Legal/compliance: Does collecting and holding community funds via the platform
   require a payment service provider license in Nigeria? (Paystack/Flutterwave may
   handle this as pass-through, but worth verifying with a lawyer.)
2. Fund flow: Does money sit in an EstateOS wallet before reaching the community
   account, or does it go direct to the estate's bank account with EstateOS taking a fee?
   Direct flow is safer and simpler for trust.
3. Gate Africa: What exactly does Gate Africa offer on the expense transparency side?
   A competitive teardown before building would sharpen differentiation.
4. Collection enforcement: What happens when a resident refuses to pay? Does the
   platform have any enforcement mechanism (service suspension, automated legal
   notice)?

## Success Criteria

- First 3 estates onboarded and using the platform within 30 days of launch
- >70% of levy payments in active estates routed through the platform
- Chairman trust improvement score (Likert 1-5, "I feel protected from false accusations")
  averages >4.0 across first 3 estates within 60 days of onboarding
- Estate chairman NPS >50 within 60 days of onboarding

### Pass 1: Information Architecture

**Screen hierarchy (visual priority order):**

**Chairman Dashboard:**
1. Alert strip — critical pending actions (pending reviews, disputes) — ALWAYS first if items exist
2. Stats grid — 4 cards: Total Collected, Total Expenses, Net Balance, Pending Reviews
3. Two-column layout (desktop): Levy Ledger (left) | Expense Log (right)
4. Trust banner with public link — always visible, not hidden in settings
5. Single column on mobile (levy ledger then expense log then trust banner)

**Navigation flow (chairman):**
```
Dashboard → [Levy detail → Resident list → Mark paid / Review transfer]
         → [Add Expense → Upload receipt → Confirm (double-confirm >₦200k)]
         → [Settings → Manage residents → CSV import → Levy types]
```

**Resident Portal:**
1. Hero card — Unit ID, name, total owed (own status at a glance)
2. Outstanding levies — MOST URGENT: anything unpaid or overdue
3. Community Financial Summary — "where did the money go?" (moved above payment history)
4. Payment History (personal record)
5. Estate Expense Log (full transparency)

Rationale: A resident's first question when opening the app is "what do I owe?" — hero answers that. Their second question is "is the estate being managed honestly?" — the community balance answers that. Payment history is reference, not primary.

**Public Summary (no-login):**
1. Top badge — "No login required · Share in WhatsApp" (context-setting)
2. Hero — estate name, 3 numbers (collected / spent / balance) — full picture in 3 seconds
3. Trust banner — explains permanence and immutability
4. Collection progress for current active levy
5. Community balance detail (collected + spent + net)
6. Spending by category
7. Recent expenses table
8. Footer with EstateOS CTA

**Navigation between tiers:** No cross-linking. Chairman app ≠ resident app ≠ public page. Distinct URL namespaces: `/chairman/...`, `/resident/...`, `/public/:token`.
